What Is Pinterest and How Does It Work? A Beginner's Guide

Pinterest is a visual bookmarking site. You collect images that interest you — recipes, outfits, room layouts, design references — and pin them to boards you can come back to. It looks a bit like a social network, but most people use it more like a search engine for ideas than a place to post about their day.

Pins and boards, the two things to understand

A pin is a single image, usually linking back to the site it came from: a blog post, a shop, a recipe. A board is a collection of pins you group under a theme, like "kitchen ideas" or "brand references". You save pins to boards, and over time a board becomes your own curated set of images on that subject. Boards can also be split into sections once they grow.

The home feed and how it decides what to show

Your home feed is a stream of pins Pinterest thinks you'll like, based on what you've saved, searched, and clicked. Save a few pins about ceramics and the feed leans that way within a session or two. It's less about who you follow and more about what you've shown interest in, which is why two people's feeds look nothing alike.

Search is where it's strongest

Most people come to Pinterest with something specific in mind and search for it. Type a term, and you get a wall of related images you can filter down by style, color, or type. There's a full guide to searching well, but the short version is that Pinterest rewards specific searches and the filter chips at the top of the results.

What people actually use it for

The common threads are planning and gathering. People use it to collect ideas ahead of a project — a wedding, a renovation, a rebrand — and to shop, since many pins link straight to a product. Designers and other creatives lean on it as a reference library. It's less about broadcasting and more about quietly building collections you'll use later.

Keeping the pins you care about

Because a pin lives on Pinterest and links out to a site that might change, the ideas you gather aren't really yours until you save them as files. Once you've built a board worth keeping, Revapin downloads the pins straight to your computer as images, folders, or a PDF, so your collection survives a deleted pin or a dead link.

How it's different from Instagram or TikTok

Instagram and TikTok are built around a feed of the latest posts from accounts and creators. Pinterest is built around search and saving, and a good pin keeps getting found months after it went up. You're not really there to keep up with people; you're there to find and file away ideas, which is a calmer way to use it.

Start with one board on something you're actually planning, save a handful of pins to it, and the rest of Pinterest makes sense quickly. It's a tool for collecting ideas, and it gets more useful the more you shape it around what you care about.
