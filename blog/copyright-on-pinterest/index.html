Copyright on Pinterest: What You Can and Can't Reuse

The images on Pinterest belong to whoever made them, not to Pinterest and not to you for saving them. You can keep them for personal reference, but reusing them publicly or selling them is a different matter and usually needs permission.

Who actually owns a pin

A pin is a copy of an image that lives somewhere else, with a link back to the source. The person who photographed, drew, or designed it holds the copyright the moment they make it. Pinning it, saving it, or seeing it on a board changes none of that. So the picture on your board isn't yours; it's someone else's work you've bookmarked.

Personal reference is fine

Keeping images to look at, plan with, or gather ideas from is normal and low-risk. A moodboard for your own kitchen, a folder of styles you like, a set of references while you sketch — that's private use, and it isn't the kind of thing copyright is meant to stop. The rights stay with the creator; you're just consulting the work, not passing it off or profiting from it.

Where reuse crosses a line

The trouble starts when the image leaves your private space. Reposting it as if it were yours, putting it on a product you sell, using it in an ad, or building a client project around it are all uses that can infringe the creator's rights. "I found it on Pinterest" is not a license. If you want to use someone's image publicly or commercially, you need their permission, or a proper license, or you need to use work that's actually cleared for that — stock with the right license, or your own images.

Keeping references private on your own machine

The cleanest way to stay on the right side of this is to keep your reference images where they belong: private, on your own computer, for your eyes. Scattered across boards, it's easy to lose track of what was just inspiration and what you started treating as usable. A simple way to keep it straight:

  • private planning and inspiration — generally fine;
  • reposting publicly or claiming it as your own — needs permission;
  • anything commercial, from products to ads — needs a license.

None of this is legal advice, and rules vary by country. When you're unsure, treat the image as off-limits for public use until the creator says otherwise. Reference all you want; publishing and selling are where you ask first.
