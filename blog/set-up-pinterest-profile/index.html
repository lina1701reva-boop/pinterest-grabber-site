How to Set Up Your Pinterest Profile for Maximum Impact

Your Pinterest profile is the first thing a new visitor reads before deciding to follow you, and most people fill it out in thirty seconds and never touch it again. A few deliberate choices — the name field, the bio, and which boards show first — do most of the work of turning a passerby into a follower. Here's what to put where.

Photo and display name

Use a clear headshot if you're a person, or a clean logo if you're a brand. Small, busy images turn to mush at profile size, so keep it simple. The display name is where a lot of people waste their best keywords — you have room for more than just your name. Put what you do after it, like "Mara Lewis · Watercolor Prints," because that text is searchable and tells people what they'll get.

Writing the bio

The bio has one job: say who you help and with what, in plain words someone would actually type into search. Skip the vague inspirational lines. Three specific details beat a paragraph of adjectives.

  • what you make or write about;
  • who it's for;
  • one reason to follow, like weekly new pins or free templates.

Verify your website

If you have a site, add it and verify it through Pinterest's settings. Verification puts a small globe icon next to your domain, gives you analytics on pins from that site, and makes your profile look established rather than empty. It's a one-time DNS or HTML step, and it changes how people read the rest of your page.

Keep a copy of your boards

Once your profile is doing its job, the boards behind it become real work — hours of curation you'd hate to lose to a deleted pin or a source that goes dark. A browser extension can pull a whole board down to your drive; Revapin does that, saving your boards as image folders you own outright.
