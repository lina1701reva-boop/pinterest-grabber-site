Why We Built Revapin: The Story Behind the Extension

We built Revapin because saving pins by hand is miserable and every shortcut felt wrong. We wanted whole boards, at full resolution, on our own computers, without pasting links into a stranger's website or settling for a blurry thumbnail. Nothing did that the way we wanted, so we made it.

The part that drove us up the wall

It started with a board of references for a project. A couple hundred pins, each one worth keeping, and the only way to save them was to open each pin, right-click, save, repeat. An hour in, we had a folder half full of images that turned out to be compressed previews, soft the moment you opened them at size. The good version was somewhere on Pinterest's servers, and there was no button that just gave it to us.

Everything else was worse

So we looked at the alternatives. Paste-a-link download sites wanted the URL of every pin, one at a time, and half of them buried the download under ads or asked you to sign up. The ones that offered bulk downloads wanted you to hand over your account, or they ran everything through their own servers, which meant your boards passed through someone you'd never met. For images we were keeping precisely because they mattered, that felt like the wrong trade.

What we actually wanted

The list was short. We wanted to select a whole board and get every pin, at the resolution Pinterest actually stored, in one go. We wanted the sections we'd already organized to stay organized. And we wanted it to happen on our own machine, so nothing was uploaded anywhere and no account credentials changed hands. A few clear things, none of which existed together in one place.

Building it to run in your own browser

That's the shape Revapin took. It's a browser extension, which means it works right there on the Pinterest page you're already looking at. When you download a board, the files go from Pinterest to your computer without a middleman server in between. Nothing gets uploaded, your account stays yours, and the whole thing runs inside the Chrome window you already trust. Here's what mattered most to us:

  • Full-resolution files, not the previews a right-click gives you;
  • Whole boards in one pass, sections kept as folders;
  • Everything local, so nothing routes through an outside site.

Who it turned out to be for

We built it for ourselves, and it turns out a lot of people had the same afternoon we did. Designers pulling references into a project folder. Creators turning boards into moodboards. Anyone who's watched a pin they saved disappear because whoever posted it took it down, and decided they'd rather keep their own copy. If you've ever right-clicked your way through a board and ended up with soft thumbnails, this was made for that.

Revapin exists because the tools we could find asked for too much and gave back too little. We wanted our boards, in full quality, kept somewhere private. So we made the thing that does that, and we kept it simple enough that it stays out of your way.
