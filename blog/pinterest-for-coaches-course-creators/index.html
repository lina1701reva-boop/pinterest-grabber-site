Pinterest for Coaches and Course Creators

Pinterest works for coaches and course creators because people search it for answers, and your free guide or course is one of those answers. A pin you make this month can keep sending signups a year from now, which fits the long sales cycle most coaching offers have.

Send pins to a lead magnet, not your homepage

The point of a pin is the click, and the click should land on a page with one job. Link to your free workbook, quiz, or webinar signup rather than a busy homepage. Someone who taps a pin about meal planning wants the meal-planning freebie, and a clean landing page gets more of them onto your list.

Write for search, because Pinterest is a search engine

People type real phrases into Pinterest. Match them. Put the terms your audience searches into pin titles, descriptions, and board names. Think about the words a beginner would use, not the industry jargon you'd use with peers. A pin titled for how someone actually phrases their problem gets found by the people who have it.

Lean on evergreen topics

Coaching content ages slowly, and Pinterest rewards that. A pin about setting boundaries or building a morning routine stays relevant for years, so it keeps earning saves and clicks long after it goes up. Build boards around themes that don't expire:

  • the core problems your ideal client keeps running into;
  • step-by-step how-tos tied to your method;
  • mindset and habit topics your niche searches often.

A back catalogue of evergreen pins does the compounding work while you focus on delivery.

Study creators already winning your niche

The coaches ranking for your keywords have already tested what pulls clicks. Their pin design, hooks, and freebie framing are on display in search. Saving a set of them gives you a reference file to study offline instead of squinting at the feed. A download extension like Revapin pulls those pins to your computer, so you can build a swipe file of opt-in pins that work and pull from it while you design your own.
