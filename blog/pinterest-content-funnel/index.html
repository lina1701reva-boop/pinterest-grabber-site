Building a Content Funnel: Pinterest to Landing Page to Sale

A Pinterest content funnel moves someone from a pin to a landing page to a sale, with each step matching the one before it. The pin makes a promise, the landing page keeps it, and the offer closes it. When those three line up, casual searchers turn into buyers.

The pin sets the promise

The funnel starts with a pin that catches a searcher. It has to speak to a real want — a recipe, a template, a way to solve something — and it has to rank for the keyword those people type. The pin's job is one click: get the right person to your page. Nothing more.

The landing page keeps it

The biggest leak is a mismatch here. If the pin says "free meal prep printable" and the page is your homepage, people bounce. The landing page has to deliver exactly what the pin promised, right at the top, with one clear next step. Match the wording, match the image, match the intent.

The offer closes it

Once someone's on the page and warm, the offer follows naturally. Sometimes it's an immediate sale; more often it's an email signup that lets you warm them further before asking for money. The three pieces of a funnel that actually converts:

  • a pin that ranks for what buyers search;
  • a landing page that delivers the pin's exact promise;
  • an offer sized to how warm the visitor is.

Study funnels of successful accounts

The quickest way to learn funnel-building is to trace one that works. Find an account selling well, click its pins, and see where they lead.
