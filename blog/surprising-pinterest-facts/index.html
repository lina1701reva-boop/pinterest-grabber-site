15 Surprising Facts About Pinterest You Probably Didn't Know

Most people think of Pinterest as a place to save recipes and outfits. It's stranger and more useful than that. Here are fifteen facts about Pinterest that reframe how the platform actually works, from visual search to the way people plan purchases on it.

It's a search engine wearing a social skin

People often lump Pinterest in with social apps, but most activity starts with a search, not a feed of friends. That changes everything about how content gets found. A few facts that follow from it:

  • you can search Pinterest with an image, not just words, using its visual search;
  • a pin can keep surfacing in results months or years after it's posted;
  • board and pin names work like keywords, the way page titles do on the web.

Once you see Pinterest as search-first, the rest of these make sense.

People come to plan, not to scroll

Pinterest tends to catch people in a planning frame of mind. They're mapping out a wedding, a room, a trip, or a project, and they save ideas to come back to. A large share of users say they use it to plan purchases, which is why the audience skews closer to buying than browsing. Users also save things well ahead of the moment, so seasonal searches climb weeks before the event itself.

The reach is bigger and quieter than you'd guess

Pinterest has a very large user base spread across many countries, and a growing chunk of it now sits outside the United States. It quietly ranks among the more-used platforms worldwide without the drama of the bigger social networks. The audience also isn't the narrow stereotype some people carry, since men and younger users have both become a meaningful part of it over the years.

Pins don't live forever, so save the ones you love

For all its long content life, nothing on Pinterest is permanent. Accounts get deleted, pins get taken down, and boards vanish, which means an image you saved can be gone when you go looking for it. Keeping the pins that matter to you as files on your own computer is the fix. A download extension like Revapin saves individual pins or whole boards to your computer, so the references and ideas you care about stay yours even if the pin disappears.
