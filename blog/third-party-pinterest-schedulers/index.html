Third-Party Scheduling Tools for Pinterest: Pros and Cons

A dedicated scheduler like Tailwind lets you queue weeks of pins at once and suggests posting times, which the native Pinterest scheduler can't do. The trade is cost and one more account to manage. If you pin at real volume it pays off; if you post a few times a week, the built-in tool is plenty.

What the native scheduler misses

Pinterest's own scheduler lets you set one pin at a time, only about two weeks ahead. That's fine when you're small. Once you want to load a month of pins in a sitting, doing it one by one gets tedious fast, and that's the gap third-party tools fill.

What a dedicated tool adds

Tools built for Pinterest give you more room to work:

  • bulk upload and queue dozens of pins at once;
  • suggested times based on when your audience is active;
  • a calendar view so you can see gaps and holidays ahead.

For a business pinning daily across many boards, those features save real hours.

The cost and the catch

These tools run a monthly fee, and that's the honest downside. There's a second one: it's easy to lean on automation until your pinning feels robotic. Spraying the same pin across twenty boards in an hour looks like spam to Pinterest and can hurt your reach. Use the scheduling, skip the aggressive loop features.

Build a local library to schedule from

Whatever scheduler you use, feeding it is smoother when your images live in folders on your computer instead of scattered across tabs. Revapin downloads reference pins and your own boards into sorted folders, so when you sit down to fill a month's queue you're pulling from a library you already own rather than hunting for each image first.
