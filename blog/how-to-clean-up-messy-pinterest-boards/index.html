How to Clean Up Messy Pinterest Boards

A board that grew past a few hundred pins stops being useful. You saved everything that caught your eye, and now finding the one pin you need means scrolling forever. Cleaning it up comes down to three things: splitting into sections, cutting the dead weight, and deciding what actually belongs together.

Back up before you start deleting

A cleanup means removing pins, and it's easy to cut something you later wish you'd kept. Saving a copy of the board first takes that risk off the table. Revapin downloads the whole board to your computer in one pass, so even if you delete a pin during the cleanup, the image is still sitting in a folder. It's free to add, and doing this first is the cheap insurance that makes the rest less nerve-wracking.

Split the board into sections

Adding sections and moving pins into them helps more than anything else you can do. A 300-pin board becomes navigable the moment it's four or five labelled groups instead of one pile. Pinterest lets you create a section and drag pins in, though for a large board it's slow, so it helps to do it in a few sittings rather than all at once.

Cut the dead weight

Some pins aren't worth keeping, and clearing them makes everything else easier to see:

  • duplicates you saved twice without noticing;
  • pins whose link is broken or leads nowhere useful;
  • things you saved on impulse and no longer care about.

You don't have to be ruthless, but a board carrying a third less clutter is a board you'll actually open.

Merge or split boards

Sometimes the mess is at the board level, not inside one board. Two half-used boards on the same topic are better merged into one with sections. A single board that's quietly become three different subjects is better split apart. Pinterest doesn't merge boards directly, so the practical route is to move pins from one into a section of the other.

Keep it from happening again

The reason boards get messy is that saving is easy and sorting is a separate task you keep putting off. Making a section the moment a board hits a natural split, instead of waiting until it's unmanageable, keeps you out of the same cleanup next year.

Back it up, break it into sections, and cut what you won't miss. A board you can actually navigate is worth the hour it takes to get there.
