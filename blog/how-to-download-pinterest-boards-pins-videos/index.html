How to Download Pinterest Boards, Pins & Videos (2026 Guide)

After a few months of pinning, most people hit the same wall. Everything they've collected still lives inside Pinterest, and there's no clear way to pull it out as real files. You can save a pin to another board, but you can't save it to your desktop the way you'd save a photo from any ordinary website. Here's how people actually get their images and videos out, and where each method falls short.

When exporting pins makes sense

People download their pins for fairly ordinary reasons. A designer building a moodboard wants the images available offline, without a browser tab open across three meetings. Someone planning a renovation wants their kitchen references in a folder they can flip through on site, where the wifi is bad. Plenty of people simply don't trust that a pin they love will still be there next year, because links rot and accounts vanish. And if you've spent an evening carefully sorting references into sections, doing anything real with them later tends to start with getting them off Pinterest first.

Saving pins one at a time

You can right-click most pins and choose “Save image as.” Saving a handful this way is manageable. A board with a few hundred pins is a different matter, and it turns into dozens or hundreds of repetitive downloads, each landing with a cryptic filename. There's a quieter problem underneath it too: the image shown on the board is often a smaller preview, not the original, so the file you save can look softer than what was on screen. You usually only notice after opening it and trying to zoom in.

Downloading many pins at once

Browser extensions handle this by adding download controls directly inside Pinterest. Revapin follows that approach. You tick the pins you want, or grab a whole board in one action, then choose whether to save them as a ZIP, a plain folder, or a single PDF. The files go to your Downloads folder at full resolution, and because everything runs in your browser, you're not pasting board links into a third-party site or uploading anything.

Videos and idea pins

Videos are the awkward case. Pinterest gives them no save button and right-clicking leads nowhere useful, since the video is streamed in pieces rather than served as one file. Some extensions can pull the original video and hand it back as a usable MP4. Revapin treats a video pin the same as an image, so it comes down with everything else. Idea pins are their own thing, closer to a slideshow of separate pages, and each video page has to be pulled individually. There's a separate guide on saving Pinterest videos if that's mostly what you're after.

Whole boards, sections, and profiles

If your boards are split into sections, it's worth keeping that structure once they're on your computer. Some tools save each section as its own folder instead of tipping everything into one pile, and can pull an entire profile when you want the whole thing. Large boards get split into several archives on the way down, which keeps the browser from running out of memory halfway through a big export.

Image quality

Quality is the thing people notice last and regret first. Pinterest shows a downscaled preview in the feed so it loads quickly, so a pin that looked sharp while scrolling can turn grainy once it's a file and you open it full size. Pulling the source image sidesteps that. It matters most for anything you plan to print, or a texture you'll blow up in a design.

Reusing Pinterest content

Downloading for your own reference is normal, though the images still belong to whoever made them. Keep saved pins for private inspiration, credit creators when you post something publicly, and don't pass someone else's work off as your own. If you're pulling material for client work, that line gets more important, and Pinterest's own terms are worth a read before you build a deliverable on top of borrowed content.
